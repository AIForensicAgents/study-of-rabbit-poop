# study-of-rabbit-poop

A lightweight, educational repository documenting a field-study approach to rabbit scat (droppings, feces, cecotropes) for biology students, naturalists, and curious field ecologists. Scientific rigor with a wink — because science can be precise and fun.

---

## Overview

This project collects observational notes, photographic records, and practical field guidance for recognizing and interpreting rabbit droppings in the wild. It is intended as an educational resource for:

- Undergraduate and graduate field courses
- Citizen science and naturalist training
- Preliminary reconnaissance in habitat and diet studies

This repository is not a substitute for veterinary advice or formal lab protocols — it's a field guide and study framework.

---

## Why it matters

- Rabbit scat is an accessible, non-invasive data source for studying diet, habitat use, and population presence.
- Pellet counts and spatial distribution help estimate relative abundance and activity patterns.
- Morphology and content (e.g., seeds, fur, undigested fiber) provide quick proxies for diet composition and seasonal shifts.
- Understanding scat helps inform habitat restoration, pest management, and biodiversity surveys without needing to trap or stress animals.

## Morphology notes

Key terms:
- **Pellets**: typically round to ovoid, dry, and separate; commonly seen from cottontails and many lagomorphs.
- **Cecotropes (soft feces)**: nutrient-rich, soft, often consumed by the rabbit directly from the anus; rarely found intact in the environment unless expelled.

Observational features to record:
- **Size** (mm): length and diameter of representative pellets.
- **Shape**: spherical, ovoid, elongated; presence of a "point."
- **Color and sheen**: can indicate freshness and diet (greenish = recent plant-heavy diet; dark brown/black = more mature/woody diet).
- **Texture**: smooth, fibrous, crumbly, mucous-coated (cecotropes).
- **Moisture/freshness**: note time since deposition when possible.
- **Associated signs**: tracks, fur, feeding evidence (nibbled stems, clipped grass).

Sampling tip: photograph with a scale (ruler or coin) and note compass-bearing and GPS coordinate. Always favor photography over collection unless you have permits and proper lab protocols.

## Habitat / Diet clues

Interpreting droppings in context:

- **High-fiber, dry pellets**: indicate a grass/forb-heavy diet (meadows, agricultural edges).
- **Feces with seed fragments or fruit residues**: suggest foraging on shrubs, berry patches, or human-associated foods.
- **Green, moist pellets or cecotrope remnants**: often reflect fresh browse, early spring growth, or soft leafy diets.
- **Wood fiber, darker color**: seasonal shift to woody browse or older plant material (fall/winter).
- **Spatial patterns**:
  - Concentrated pellet piles near burrows/latrines can mark home ranges (especially in some species).
  - Dispersed pellets along trails indicate transit corridors and feeding routes.

Combine scat data with vegetation surveys and trail cameras for stronger inferences.

## Ethics / Safety

Professional and ethical field conduct is essential.

Field ethics:
- **Do not disturb** animals or their shelters to collect scat.
- **Photograph in situ** whenever possible. Only collect samples with proper permits and Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) or equivalent approvals when required.
- **Minimize habitat impact**: avoid trampling sensitive vegetation or creating new trails.

Biosecurity and safety:
- Always use gloves (nitrile/latex) when handling droppings and field tools.
- Avoid touching face, eyes, mouth while working; wash hands thoroughly with soap and water after fieldwork.
- Disinfect tools and surfaces that contact samples.
- Be aware that wild lagomorphs can harbor pathogens (e.g., bacterial agents and parasites). If you require lab analysis, follow your institution's biosafety protocols.
- If you find sick or obviously abnormal animals or multiple dead animals, report to local wildlife authorities — do not handle carcasses.

Legal notes:
- Respect private property and protected lands; obtain permission and permits as required.
- Follow local wildlife and collection regulations.

## Future work

Planned directions to expand scientific value:

- Develop a searchable field-key for pellet morphology (size, color, texture).
- Pilot studies correlating pellet metrics with nutritional analyses (C:N ratios, fiber).
- Microbiome and parasitology surveys (with institutional collaboration and permits).
- Machine learning classifier for pellet photos to support citizen scientists.
- A lightweight mobile app for geo-tagged scat observations and automated data submission.
